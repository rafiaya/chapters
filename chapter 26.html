Chapter 26: Space Telescopes

Introduction

Space telescopes are observatories placed beyond Earth’s atmosphere to capture clearer and more detailed images of the universe. Unlike ground-based telescopes, they avoid distortion caused by Earth’s air, giving astronomers sharper views of distant stars, galaxies, and planets.

Why Space Telescopes?

  • Eliminate atmospheric distortion and light pollution.
  • Observe in wavelengths (like ultraviolet, X-ray, infrared) that cannot pass through Earth’s atmosphere.
  • Provide continuous, uninterrupted observations of cosmic phenomena.

Famous Space Telescopes

  • Hubble Space Telescope (1990–present): Provided iconic deep-space images and helped determine the universe’s expansion rate.
  • Chandra X-ray Observatory (1999–present): Observes high-energy regions like black holes and supernovae.
  • Spitzer Space Telescope (2003–2020): Specialized in infrared astronomy, revealing exoplanets and dusty regions of galaxies.
  • James Webb Space Telescope (2021–present): The most advanced space telescope, studying the first galaxies, exoplanet atmospheres, and the origins of stars and planets.

Scientific Contributions

  • Measuring the age and size of the universe.
  • Capturing images of galaxies billions of light-years away.
  • Discovering thousands of exoplanets and analyzing their atmospheres.
  • Revealing how stars and planetary systems form.

Future Space Telescopes

  • Roman Space Telescope: Designed to study dark energy and exoplanets with a wide-field view.
  • LUVOIR (concept): A next-generation telescope capable of directly imaging Earth-like exoplanets.

Conclusion

Space telescopes have transformed astronomy by opening windows into parts of the universe once hidden from us. They remain essential tools for solving cosmic mysteries and inspiring humanity’s quest for knowledge.
