Chapter 6: The International Space Station (ISS)

Introduction

The International Space Station (ISS) is the largest human-made structure in space, orbiting Earth every 90 minutes. It serves as a microgravity laboratory where astronauts and scientists from around the world conduct experiments that cannot be done on Earth.

History

The ISS was launched in 1998 as a joint project involving NASA, Roscosmos (Russia), ESA (Europe), JAXA (Japan), and CSA (Canada). It represents one of the greatest international collaborations in history.

Structure

The station consists of interconnected modules, solar panels, and large trusses. Modules provide living quarters, laboratories, and docking ports for spacecraft.

Scientific Contributions

  • Medical research: Understanding how the human body reacts to long-term space travel.
  • Physics experiments: Studying fluids, combustion, and materials in microgravity.
  • Earth observation: Monitoring natural disasters, climate change, and atmospheric conditions.
  • Technology testing: Preparing for future missions to the Moon and Mars.

Life on the ISS

Future

The ISS is expected to operate until at least 2030, after which commercial space stations may take over. Its legacy will continue as a foundation for deeper space exploration.
