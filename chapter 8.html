Chapter 8: Space Telescopes and Observatories

Introduction

Space telescopes are powerful instruments placed above Earth’s atmosphere to observe the universe without atmospheric distortion. Unlike ground-based observatories, they provide clearer and more detailed images of distant galaxies, stars, and planets.

The Hubble Space Telescope

Launched in 1990, the Hubble Space Telescope revolutionized astronomy with its breathtaking images. It has captured galaxies billions of light-years away, studied the life cycle of stars, and helped determine the age of the universe.

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)

Launched in 2021, JWST is the most advanced telescope ever built. With its massive gold-coated mirror and infrared instruments, it looks deeper into space than ever before. It studies the first galaxies formed after the Big Bang and searches for signs of life on distant exoplanets.

Other Important Observatories

  • Chandra X-ray Observatory: Studies black holes, neutron stars, and high-energy cosmic events.
  • Spitzer Space Telescope: Used infrared light to study exoplanets and galaxies (retired in 2020).
  • Kepler Space Telescope: Discovered thousands of exoplanets by observing stars’ light dimming when planets passed in front.

Scientific Impact

These observatories have expanded our understanding of the universe. They revealed dark matter, dark energy, and helped scientists locate potentially habitable exoplanets. Their discoveries inspire new missions and shape future exploration.

Conclusion

Space telescopes like Hubble and JWST are humanity’s cosmic eyes, peering deep into the universe. They allow us to look back in time, uncovering secrets of the cosmos and paving the way for the next generation of exploration.
