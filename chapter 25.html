Chapter 25: Space Stations

Introduction

Space stations are large spacecraft in orbit that serve as living and working environments for astronauts over long durations. They enable scientific research, technological testing, and international collaboration in space.

Early Space Stations

  • Salyut (1971): The Soviet Union launched the world’s first space station, used for military and scientific missions.
  • Skylab (1973): NASA’s first space station, where astronauts conducted solar and Earth studies.
  • Mir (1986–2001): A Soviet/Russian station that supported long-term human habitation in orbit.

International Space Station (ISS)

The ISS is a joint project involving NASA, Roscosmos, ESA, JAXA, and CSA. Since 2000, it has been continuously inhabited, hosting astronauts from around the world. The ISS serves as a laboratory for biology, physics, medicine, and Earth observation.

Scientific Contributions

  • Studies on the effects of microgravity on the human body.
  • Development of new technologies for space and Earth applications.
  • Testing of life-support systems for future deep-space missions.

Future Space Stations

  • Lunar Gateway: Planned by NASA and international partners as part of the Artemis program, orbiting the Moon.
  • Chinese Tiangong: China’s space station, operational and expanding with new modules.
  • Commercial Stations: Companies like Axiom Space are developing private space stations for research and tourism.

Conclusion

Space stations symbolize international cooperation and human endurance. They prepare us for future exploration of the Moon, Mars, and beyond while expanding scientific knowledge in unique ways only possible in orbit.
